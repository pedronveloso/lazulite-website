## Design Context

### Users
Advanced Android tech users and audiophiles who care deeply about Bluetooth audio quality. They're informed, skeptical, and data-driven — they don't accept "hi-res audio" claims at face value and want actual signal data. They visit the site to understand what the app does before downloading, or to get setup help via the FAQ. They likely arrive from audiophile forums, Reddit, or searching for Bluetooth codec analysis tools.

### Brand Personality
Technical, Elite, Well-crafted. Lazulite is the tool that exposes what streaming apps and headphone manufacturers don't tell you. It's for people who know what LDAC means and care that it's being negotiated correctly. The brand is not for casual listeners — it's for those who obsess over codec fidelity.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Dark theme** (audiophiles listen at night; measurement tools live in dark environments). **Amber/gold as the primary accent** — evokes VU meters, tube amp warmth, and precision instrument displays, NOT neon gaming glows. Deep cool-dark slate backgrounds tinted blue-grey.

Typography: **Barlow Condensed** for display headlines (precision/aerospace/engineering feel), **Epilogue** for body text (readable, slightly editorial, refined). Think: the inside of a Chord Electronics DAC manual meets a spectrum analyzer readout.

**Anti-references**: generic SaaS dashboards, plain Material Design, Google Pixel app aesthetic, anything with cyan-on-dark / purple-to-blue neon gradients, AI product pages circa 2024.

### Design Principles
1. **Signal over noise** — every visual element earns its place; remove decoration that doesn't inform
2. **Precision, not minimalism** — the interface should feel measured and deliberate
3. **Warm authority** — amber/gold creates warmth that separates this from cold "tech bro" aesthetics
4. **Typography does the work** — big condensed type at display sizes, not generic icon grids
5. **Audiophile, not gamer** — dark theme always; neon/glow effects never
